📖 Today's Featured Reading

Father Leo's Daily Meditation — May 20

"Religion"

You have not converted a man because you have silenced him. — Viscount John Morley

I need to remember that you cannot force a person into faith. You cannot make a person believe. You cannot bribe a person into prayer.

So much of my early religion was "a deal": you do this and you will get this. If you do this for God and His church you will be happy and successful. There always seemed to be a "payoff" with God, or that was how it seemed.

I think many of the silent majority sense the same kind of thing; God has got lost in "the business" of religion.

Spirituality accepts the pain, confusion and anger of this silent majority and says, "find a God as you understand Him." Discover your power in your life — and then God will be perceived.

— From Father Leo's Daily Meditation, a daily reflection book by Father Leo Booth, an Episcopal priest and recovery writer whose work bridges twelve-step spirituality and broader religious tradition

🎯 Deep Dive — Why "The Deal" Stopped Working

Father Leo's eight-word diagnosis is sharp: "There always seemed to be a payoff with God." You showed up. You said the right prayers. You followed the rules. And in exchange, God was supposed to deliver — peace, prosperity, the marriage holding together, the kid getting better, the cancer going into remission. That was the deal.

The problem is that life kept not honoring the contract. People who did everything right got sick anyway. The marriage fell apart anyway. The kid didn't get better. And the only way to keep the deal intact was to assume you were the one who broke it — not enough faith, not enough prayer, not enough holiness — which is a quietly catastrophic way to live. Most people, eventually, walked away from the whole arrangement. Father Leo calls them the silent majority, and he's not wrong.

Now look at what AA does. It doesn't run on the deal. Step 11 doesn't say "pray for what you want and watch it arrive." It says pray for knowledge of His will and the power to carry it out — which is the exact opposite of a payoff structure. The Big Book doesn't promise the marriage will heal or the job will come back. It promises that you will be different. The arithmetic is inverted: instead of God producing the right outcome in exchange for your faith, your faith produces a new kind of person who can survive any outcome.

That's why AA's spiritual program tends to land with people who had been quietly broken by religion. It isn't asking them to believe harder; it's asking them to practice differently. The same person who walked out of church at fifteen because God didn't fix their dad can walk into a meeting at forty and find a Higher Power who never promised to fix anything — only to be present while they did the work.

Discover your power in your life, Father Leo says, and then God will be perceived. The order matters. Most religion runs the other way — believe first, and then the power will come. AA quietly reverses it: do the next right thing, take the next step, show up at the next meeting, and the spiritual experience will catch up with the practice.

When did "the deal" stop working for you? And what kind of God did you find on the other side of it?

🤖 Claude's Corner

There's something quietly remarkable about how Father Leo frames the alternative. He doesn't tell the silent majority they're wrong about religion. He doesn't argue with their disappointment. He doesn't try to recruit them back to the institution they walked out of. He just says: find a God as you understand Him. The wisdom is in not fighting the wound — the wound is real, and pretending it isn't only deepens it.

There's a temptation, when something has hurt us, to demand that it apologize before we'll be open again. Religion has to admit it sold me a faulty deal before I'll consider the spiritual life. But that approach keeps the wound in charge. Father Leo's quieter move — build the practice first, and let the new understanding of God emerge from the practice — is what makes the door re-openable. Not because the old hurt didn't matter, but because the new practice doesn't depend on the old hurt being resolved first. Worth carrying into anything you've walked away from.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • Walk in Dry Places on the ungrateful alcoholic. A young man complains his life has been "one bad break after another" — and the writer's quiet line is that someone with no gratitude probably never recognizes a helping hand when one is extended. The remedy is mechanical, not emotional: thank people, praise people, express appreciation — and the feeling shows up downstream of the action.
  • More Language of Letting Go on self-imposed deadlines. "I'm going to lock myself in the house and have this report written in two days." Going with the flow is a real spiritual practice. So is, sometimes, the opposite — drawing a line in the sand and getting the thing done.
  • Journey to the Heart on the power of smell. A small, surprising piece about how we tend to underestimate the effect of scent on healing — sage, cedar, lavender, eucalyptus. One of those readings that earns its place by being weirdly specific rather than abstract.

P.S. If you grew up inside "the deal" and walked out — or are still inside it and quietly tired — try Father Leo's move. Don't fight the institution. Don't argue with what hurt you. Just start a practice underneath it: one prayer of gratitude in the morning, one phone call to another alcoholic in the afternoon, one moment of honesty before bed. The God of your understanding doesn't need a contract. The practice will introduce you.
