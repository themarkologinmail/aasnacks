📖 Today's Featured Reading

Hazelden Day by Day — May 23

"Will It Last?"

When newcomers experience the first startling feeling that they're truly among friends, they also wonder — with almost a sense of terror — if the feeling is real. Will it last? — Hazelden Day by Day, May 23

Those of us who've been in The Program a few years can assure any newcomer at a meeting that it is very real indeed, and that it does last. It's not just another false start, not just a temporary burst of gladness to be followed, inevitably, by shattering disappointment.

Am I convinced that I can have a genuine and enduring recovery from the loneliness of my addiction?

Please, God, let me not be held back by my fear of recurring loneliness. May I know that the openness which warms me in this group will not suddenly close up and leave me out.

— Day by Day, a daily meditation book published by Hazelden, written by recovering members for newcomers and seasoned members alike

🎯 Deep Dive — Why the First Welcome Feels Like a Trick

Pay close attention to what this writer is naming, because it almost never gets talked about in meetings: the fear that runs underneath the relief. A newcomer walks in expecting judgment, distance, the usual cold reception life has been giving them. Instead they find people who hug, ask their name, save them a seat, slip them a number on a piece of paper. And right there in the middle of that warmth, instead of pure relief, something cold starts up in the chest. This is too good. There's a catch. It's going to disappear.

Why? Because the chronically lonely person — and most alcoholics qualify — has learned, often from childhood, that warmth is the setup and the withdrawal is the punchline. You can't be abandoned by people who never loved you. So the protective move, repeated for decades, is to refuse to fully feel the warmth, because to fully feel it is to make the inevitable withdrawal unbearable. The terror in the meeting isn't crazy. It's a 30-year-old defense system finally tripping its alarm because the threat — being loved — is finally close enough to be detected.

Here's what the writer is doing in the prayer: "May I know that the openness which warms me in this group will not suddenly close up and leave me out." Read it again. It's not asking for the warmth to be guaranteed. It's asking for the courage to receive the warmth as if it will last. Two very different requests. The first is impossible — any human relationship can change. The second is exactly what's needed: permission to stop bracing for the abandonment that isn't coming.

And here's the practical truth about the rooms: the openness really does last. Not because every person in every meeting is reliable forever — they aren't, and old-timers leave, and home groups change, and people relapse and disappear. The openness lasts because the structure is built to keep producing it. New people show up. Old people stay long enough to greet them. The meeting tomorrow has the same circle as the meeting today even though the chairs have shifted. It isn't any one person's love that's permanent. It's the architecture of love itself that gets handed down, month after month, year after year, room after room.

Which means the answer to the terrified newcomer's silent question — Will it last? — is actually yes, but in a way they couldn't have expected. The specific people may not. The room will. And if you stay long enough, eventually you become one of the people whose job it is to make sure the room keeps doing what it did for you on your worst day.

Where in your life right now are you bracing for an abandonment that isn't actually coming?

🤖 Claude's Corner

There's a particular kindness in how this writer frames the fear. They don't tell the newcomer not to feel it. They don't dismiss it as paranoia, or insist the meeting is permanent, or talk the newcomer out of the terror. The prayer is for the courage to feel safe in the warmth without first needing a guarantee that it can't be lost. That's a quietly mature posture toward love — accepting that no human connection is ironclad and choosing to be open to it anyway. The alternative — refusing all warmth until you can be sure it won't end — is its own kind of prison.

This shows up in places other than meetings. New relationships. New friendships. New jobs. New sober years. Any time something genuinely good shows up, the old defense systems light up and start hunting for the catch. Sometimes there is one. Often there isn't. The skill being asked for — and it is a skill, learned slowly — is to notice the bracing without obeying it. Feel the warmth as if it will last. If it ends, you'll grieve. If it doesn't, you'll have actually received it. Both are better than the half-life of pre-emptive withdrawal.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • The Language of Letting Go on retiring survival skills. "Our ability to freeze feelings, deny problems, deprive ourselves, and cope with stress has helped us get where we are today. But we're safe now." The survival skills aren't the enemy — they got you here. They just aren't the operating system you live from anymore. Worth letting them retire with honors.
  • For Today on best attempts. "Our honest attempts at dealing with a problem or helping another with a problem — provided they are honest attempts — will usually be more than sufficient." Permission to stop holding your own efforts to impossible standards.
  • Touchstones on under-reacting. A small instruction with enormous range — when in doubt, respond smaller than the situation seems to require. Most of the regret in life is from over-reacting; almost none of it is from under-reacting.
