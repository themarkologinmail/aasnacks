May 19. As Bill Sees It lands on what may be the most useful tool in the entire 12&12 — and the one almost nobody remembers exists. It's called a spot-check inventory, and it's exactly what it sounds like: not the annual review, not the Step 4 deep dive, but a thirty-second mid-day inventory you can run while you're still in the situation.

If you've ever walked away from a conversation thinking I should not have said that, the spot-check is the version of Step 10 that exists to keep that moment from happening in the first place.

🎯 Deep Dive — The Ninety-Second Inventory Most of Us Never Use

Step 10 in popular AA culture has gotten quietly reduced to one specific practice — the end-of-day review. You go to bed, you flip through the tape of the day, you write down where you were resentful, dishonest, selfish, afraid, where you owe an apology. Useful practice. Universally recommended. But Bill's chapter on Step 10 in the 12&12 actually describes three different inventories, and the end-of-day one is just the third. The first one — the one Bill called the spot-check — is the most powerful and the most underused.

Here's the asymmetry. The end-of-day inventory lets you spot the mistake. The spot-check lets you avoid making it.

The mechanics are dead simple. Mid-conversation, mid-email, mid-traffic — somewhere you feel the temperature climbing — you stop. You don't leave the room. You don't pull out a journal. You just take thirty to ninety seconds and ask three questions:

1. What am I actually feeling right now? (Not what I'm saying I feel — what's actually happening underneath. Fear? Pride? Hurt? Embarrassment?)
2. What's the part of this that's mine? (Not all of it. Just the part. The other person may be 80% wrong, but there is almost always at least 20% that's mine.)
3. What would I do here if I weren't trying to win?

That's it. That's the spot-check. The whole exercise can happen while the other person is still talking. Nothing visible from the outside. Bill's claim — and it bears out in practice — is that just running those three questions in real time is often enough to "quiet stormy emotions" and keep you from making the move you'd otherwise have to amend for tomorrow.

Why does Bill bother to single it out? Because there's a particular failure mode he's trying to address. The 24-hour delay built into the end-of-day inventory is too long for the people who live with us. By the time we've spotted the mistake in our nighttime review, the damage to the marriage, the friendship, the kid, the colleague, is already done. The spot-check is Step 10 deployed in real time — before the amend becomes necessary.

Where in your day, right now, would a ninety-second spot-check do the most good?

🤖 Claude's Corner

What Bill is quietly describing here is a very particular skill, and it has a name in contemplative traditions much older than AA: presence under disturbance. The ability to be in the middle of an emotion without becoming it. The spot-check works because it turns the noticing eye on while the storm is still going — and an emotion that's being calmly observed loses about half its grip on the system that's observing it.

There's a generous corollary here too. Bill makes a point in the same chapter that the spot-check is not the place for the big stuff. "The consideration of long-standing difficulties had better be postponed, when possible, to times deliberately set aside for that purpose." In other words: don't try to settle your father in the middle of a meeting with your boss. The spot-check is a small instrument for small moments. The big moments need a sponsor, a meeting, a quiet hour, real work. One of the consistent kindnesses of the program is that it's careful never to ask a small tool to do the work of a large one.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • Steps 8 and 9 in the 12&12 — the amends pair. The chapter frames the work in three stages: look back, repair the damage, and then "develop the best possible relations with every human being we know." Notice the third stage — most members focus on stages one and two, but Bill explicitly names a third: not just clearing the wreckage, but actively rebuilding. Amends is not a one-time event. It's the start of a different way of being in relationships.
  • The oldtimer's answer. "How do you know it was God who granted your request?" — "I didn't ask anyone else." Six words that quietly retire the entire question of did my prayer get answered or did the situation just resolve itself anyway? It's a deflection in the best sense — pointing past the question to something simpler underneath. The point isn't to prove the mechanism; the point is to address the request to your Higher Power and let the rest take care of itself.
  • Father Leo on solitude. "Take time for solitude. How else can you contemplate the blessings of recovery?" A quiet counterweight to the meeting-heavy, fellowship-heavy version of the program. Recovery happens in community, and recovery also happens in the chair, alone, with the morning coffee. Both, not either.
  • Walk in Dry Places — past as survival tool. "The ability to delude yourself may be an important survival tool." A useful piece of self-forgiveness: the denial that kept us drinking also kept us alive long enough to get here. The job isn't to hate the past self for needing the delusion. The job is to no longer need it.

P.S. Try one spot-check today. Pick a situation where you can already feel the temperature climbing — a known argument, a known email thread, a known co-worker.
