Daily Reflections — May 18

"Freedom to Be Me"

If we are painstaking about this phase of our development, we will be amazed before we are half way through. We are going to know a new freedom and a new happiness. — Alcoholics Anonymous (Fourth Edition), p. 83

My first true freedom is the freedom not to have to take a drink today. If I truly want it, I will work the Twelve Steps and the happiness of this freedom will come to me through the Steps — sometimes quickly, sometimes slowly. Other freedoms will follow, and inventorying them is a new happiness.

I am free to use the talents I have, free to think, free to enjoy God's world, free to choose, free to be wrong, free to grow, free to love, and free to be me. As I follow this path, walking towards the bright sunlight of the spirit, I gradually become the person I was meant to be — at home in my own skin, comfortable with the world around me.

— Daily Reflections, an AA Conference-approved book of meditations on the Steps, Traditions, and Big Book, written by AA members. Today's reading is on the opening line of the Ninth Step Promises (Big Book pp. 83–84).

🎯 Deep Dive — The Word in the Promises That Almost Nobody Reads Carefully

There are twelve Promises in the Big Book — or ten, or eleven, depending on how you count them — and they all start with one word that gets read over a thousand times a week in meeting rooms without ever being noticed. The word is "will."

Not might. Not could. Not if you're lucky, not some people experience, not some say that. Will. Bill W., a man who spent most of his life qualifying his statements, who softened his prose in the 12&12 specifically because he worried the Big Book had been too directive — Bill W. wrote the Promises in the future indicative. We are going to know. We will not regret. We will comprehend. We will know peace. Twelve consecutive declarative sentences making twelve consecutive non-negotiable predictions.

And then he doubles down. The paragraph ends: "Are these extravagant promises? We think not. They are being fulfilled among us — sometimes quickly, sometimes slowly. They will always materialize if we work for them." Always. Another word almost nobody hears. Bill is not hedging. He's saying: this is what happens. Reliably. Not as a metaphor, not as poetry, not as inspirational reading — but as a description of an observable outcome that the first hundred members had already produced enough times to publish a book about.

Now notice the one giant condition. The Promises appear right in the middle of the chapter on working Steps 8 and 9 — the amends steps. "If we are painstaking about this phase of our development…" The Promises are not promises about sobriety in general. They're promises about what happens to a person who has actually done the amends work — the lists, the conversations, the apologies, the restitution, the hard ones, the ones you'd rather skip. The Promises are the back-end output of Step 9. They are not free.

Which is why so many members spend years not feeling them. Not because the Promises don't work, but because the Promises are written in the future tense for a reason — they're downstream of a particular kind of work, and you don't get the harvest without the planting. Bill knew exactly what he was doing. He wrote them as a guarantee, locked behind one specific door.

Of the twelve Promises, which is the one you most want to know is true — and have you walked through the door it's behind?

🤖 Claude's Corner

The Daily Reflections piece does something beautifully simple, and worth holding onto: it lists out what freedom actually looks like. Free to use the talents I have, free to think, free to enjoy God's world, free to choose, free to be wrong, free to grow, free to love, and free to be me. Eight freedoms, in plain language. Notice that almost none of them are dramatic. None of them involve mountaintops or visions or thunderclap revelations. They're the freedoms of a Tuesday afternoon. Free to be wrong — that one alone, internalized, would change most marriages.

The promise of the Promises isn't ecstasy. It's not even, mostly, happiness in the giddy sense. It's the very specific experience of being at home in your own skin, comfortable with the world around you. An alcoholic mind, before the steps, is rarely either of those things — it's at war with itself in a body it doesn't trust, in a world it doesn't feel welcomed by. The Promises are the description of a person who has finally moved into their own life. Most of us spent decades renting from a difficult landlord. The Promises describe the deed.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • Bill on the two kinds of oldtimer. In As Bill Sees It, Bill names the fork in the road every long-timer eventually walks through: the elder statesman, who lets the group make its own decisions and sits quietly on the sidelines, and the bleeding deacon, who's convinced the group can't get along without him and conspires for re-election. Bill notes — generously — that "nearly every oldtimer in our Society has gone through this process in some degree." The hopeful line: most survive and become the former.
  • Step 7 in the 12&12. The whole step, Bill says, is on humility. "It is really saying to us that we now ought to be willing to try humility in seeking the removal of our other shortcomings just as we did when we admitted that we were powerless over alcohol." Same posture, different defect. Useful framing for any character flaw that keeps coming back: try Step 1 on it.
  • Beattie — don't put your life on hold. A precise observation: when a problem hits, alcoholics tend to suspend the rest of life and torment themselves with obsessive thoughts about the problem. "Frequently, the solution comes when we let go enough to live our life, return to our routine, and stop obsessing about the problem." Counterintuitive but consistently true.
  • Touchstones — what to do about the abyss. One of the more honest lines from the men's meditations today: "There was a concrete program, designed to secure the greatest possible inner security for us long-time escapists." The depression and dread that used to drive the drinking didn't have to be argued away — they could be put through a tested process.

P.S. Pick one Promise today — just one — and ask honestly whether you've actually experienced it. Not in flashes; sustained. If yes, that's the harvest from work you've already done. The Promises aren't aspirations to make you feel inadequate; they're a map of where the work leads.
