📖 Today's Featured Reading

Daily Reflections — May 24 — quoting Alcoholics Anonymous, p. 133

"Not a Vale of Tears"

We cannot subscribe to the belief that this life is a vale of tears, though it once was just that for many of us. But it is clear that we made our own misery. God didn't do it. Avoid then, the deliberate manufacture of misery, but if trouble comes, cheerfully capitalize it as an opportunity to demonstrate His omnipotence. — Alcoholics Anonymous (Fourth Edition), p. 133

For years I believed in a punishing God and blamed Him for my misery. I have learned that I must lay down the "weapons" of self in order to pick up the "tools" of the A.A. program.

I do not struggle with the program because it is a gift and I have never struggled when receiving a gift. If I sometimes keep on struggling, it is because I'm still hanging onto my old ideas and "the results are nil."

— Daily Reflections, an AA Conference-approved book of meditations on the Steps, Traditions, and Big Book, written by AA members; the quoted passage is from Alcoholics Anonymous (the "Big Book"), Fourth Edition, p. 133, in the chapter "The Family Afterward"

🎯 Deep Dive — The Word "Cheerfully" Is Doing a Lot of Work

This passage on page 133 is one of the more theologically interesting sentences in the Big Book, and almost nobody stops on it. Look at what Bill is doing in three short moves:

Move one: "We cannot subscribe to the belief that this life is a vale of tears." The phrase "vale of tears" — vallis lacrimarum — is a deeply traditional Christian framing, going back centuries, used to describe earthly life as a place of suffering to be endured before the real reward in the next world. Bill is, very politely, declining the whole frame. AA is not a wait-it-out program. The good is supposed to be available now, in this life, today, sober — not after death.

Move two: "But it is clear that we made our own misery. God didn't do it." This is the alcoholic mind's favorite escape hatch — God did this to me. Life did this to me. They did this to me. — being firmly closed. The Big Book is not interested in arguing about whether God is fair. It's interested in pointing out that for most of the readers, the misery in question was self-generated and self-sustained. Which is, oddly, good news: anything you made you can also unmake.

Move three — the radical one: "If trouble comes, cheerfully capitalize it as an opportunity to demonstrate His omnipotence." Read that word — cheerfully — twice. Bill isn't saying endure trouble. He isn't saying tolerate trouble, or pray your way through trouble, or have a positive attitude about trouble. He's saying when trouble shows up, treat it as raw material — capital, the word he uses — to be invested. Trouble isn't punishment to survive. It's stock to deploy.

This is the hinge of the whole AA spiritual posture, and it's why old-timers seem so unfazed by chaos that would crater most people. The phone call about the job. The diagnosis. The kid in trouble. The spouse leaving. None of these are interpreted as life punishing me. They're interpreted as material to work with. The recovery, the sponsor calls, the meetings, the prayers, the Steps — all the tools — are available specifically because trouble keeps showing up. The trouble isn't competing with the recovery. The trouble is the reason the recovery exists.

And the word "cheerfully" carries a quiet challenge. You can hate the situation and still be cheerful about how you'll meet it. Those are different things. The trouble can be terrible. Your bearing in the middle of it can still be steady, even bright. That's not denial. That's the difference between a vale of tears and a workshop.

What's the current trouble in your life that you could quietly start treating as capital instead of punishment?

🤖 Claude's Corner

There's something brave in the choice to use the word cheerfully in a recovery text. It would have been safer — and more familiar — to say "patiently capitalize it" or "humbly capitalize it." The book chooses cheerfully, which is a much less ornamental virtue. Patience and humility can be performed. Cheerfulness has to be felt, or at least practiced toward. It puts the bar somewhere unexpected: not just bearing trouble well, but bearing it without a long face. The Big Book is, in this small word, asking for something close to actual joy in adversity — which is a startlingly high spiritual ask, and worth honoring as the ambition it is, not watering down into something smaller.

It's worth noticing too what this passage isn't doing. It isn't promising that life won't hurt. It isn't claiming the trouble doesn't matter. It isn't suggesting that the right attitude makes problems disappear. It's saying something much more modest and useful: the situation is what it is, and the only thing in your control is what kind of person shows up to meet it. That part — the part that's in your control — is enough. Always was. Worth carrying into anything heavy you're holding right now.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • Walk in Dry Places on real vs. fake guidance. The honest warning: "We can easily fall into serious error by assuming that our own impulses and prejudices are 'the will of God' for ourselves and others." A useful sober check on the temptation to baptize your own preferences. Real guidance, the writer notes, usually arrives with a sense of peace and rightness — not urgency, not vindication, not the rush of being proven right.
  • More Language of Letting Go on the friend stuck in "firsts." A small, recognizable portrait of someone who's been about to start their writing project for years — "I'll start, but first I've got to learn this program. First I've got to read this book." The mask of preparation hiding the fear of beginning. Worth checking your own current "firsts."
  • Dietrich Bonhoeffer's line. "Action springs not from thought, but from a readiness for responsibility." The Bonhoeffer angle is worth knowing — this is the German theologian executed by the Nazis for resistance work, writing about action under the most extreme pressure imaginable. Coming from him, the line has weight.
