📖 Today's Featured Reading

For Today (Overeaters Anonymous) — May 22

"The First Word"

The journey to find God and to do His will one day at a time began with the first word of the First Step … "We." — For Today, May 22

When I was drinking all I could ever think about was "I, I, I," or "Me, Me, Me." Such painful obsessions of self, such soul sickness, such spiritual selfishness bound me to the bottle for more than half my life.

The journey to find God and to do His will one day at a time began with the first word of the First Step … "We."

There was power in numbers, there was strength in numbers, there was safety in numbers, and for an alcoholic like me, there was life in numbers. If I had tried to recover alone I probably would have died.

With God and another alcoholic I have a divine purpose in my life … I have become a channel for God's healing love.

— For Today, a daily meditation book published by Overeaters Anonymous, drawing on the same twelve-step framework as AA and structured around shared experience from members

🎯 Deep Dive — Why Step One Doesn't Start With "I"

Take a second and read the first word of every Step. Not the verb. The pronoun. Every single one of them, all twelve, opens with the same word: We. Not "I admitted." Not "I came to believe." We admitted. We came to believe. We made a decision.

This is not stylistic. Bill W. and the early members chose that word with care, and they chose it in the face of a counter-pressure most people don't realize was there: the alcoholic mind is the most stubbornly first-person mind alive. The Big Book is brutally precise about this. "Selfishness — self-centeredness! That, we think, is the root of our troubles." (p. 62.) The disease produces an entire grammar of one. My drinking. My problem. My reasons. My life. "I, I, I" or "Me, Me, Me," as today's writer puts it.

And what does the program do on the very first move? Before it asks anything else of you — before powerlessness, before God, before inventory — it changes the pronoun. The Step doesn't say "you are powerless." It doesn't even say "I am powerless." It says we admitted we were powerless. The cure for the disease of being alone with yourself begins, structurally, by no longer being alone with yourself in the very sentence you're reciting.

This is why working the Steps in isolation almost never works. People try. People read the Big Book on their own, do written inventories alone in a notebook, never share with another human, and wonder why nothing's shifting. The grammar didn't change. The whole point of the Step is that you don't admit anything alone. You admit it inside a "we." You take the inventory and you read it to someone else (Step 5). You ask for the defects to be removed and you ask in the company of others doing the same thing (Steps 6 and 7). The program is one long act of dragging a first-person sentence into the first-person plural.

Today's writer is blunt: "If I had tried to recover alone I probably would have died." Most alcoholics will recognize that as not even slightly hyperbolic. The disease lives in the "I." The recovery lives in the "we." And the entire program is engineered to keep moving you, sentence by sentence, from one to the other.

Where in your program are you still trying to do it alone? Whose "we" do you belong to right now?

🤖 Claude's Corner

There's a small but profound thing happening in the choice of "we" that's worth naming. When you say "I am powerless," the sentence puts you alone with the powerlessness — the powerlessness is yours, the burden of bearing it is yours, the shame of admitting it is yours. When you say "we are powerless," the same fact becomes a piece of shared information about a category you belong to. It stops being a personal flaw and becomes a description of a condition. Nothing about the powerlessness changes. Everything about how it sits in you does.

There's a wider thread here too. So much of the difficulty of being human comes from the conviction that the bad thing you're carrying is uniquely yours. The grief is yours. The fear is yours. The failure mode is yours. Most religious and contemplative traditions have noticed, in their own way, that this is largely an illusion of perspective. The plural pronoun is one of the simplest spiritual technologies ever invented. It does almost no work on the situation; it does enormous work on how the situation is held. Worth holding alongside anything you're currently carrying in the first person singular.

📋 Also Worth Knowing Today

A few other things from today's readings, if you want them:

  • Twenty-Four Hours a Day on radical sharing. The thing that impresses most about a meeting is the willingness to share without holding anything back. Worth noticing as you sit in a room today — most spaces in life run on the opposite principle. The meeting is one of the few rooms where the curated version is the wrong move.
  • Touchstones on the perfectionism trap. "Those of us who go around trying to be right and do everything right are likely to betray ourselves. We stifle our impulses and control our intuition because we can't be certain that we are correct." One of those lines that quietly reframes a lifelong pattern as a form of self-betrayal rather than virtue.
  • The Language of Letting Go on the cycle of need. The "we have to act as if before the belief catches up" pattern — useful for anyone with childhood programming that says you don't deserve good things. The mechanism of recovery is often: act as if you do, watch the evidence accumulate, and let the belief shift slowly.
